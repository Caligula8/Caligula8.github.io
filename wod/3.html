“The United States does not wage war on their citizens [...] these individuals must be helped” the ONDCP goes on to say. And yet, in a meta analysis on why so many people are being or have been incarcerated, studies show that violent crime rates are going down, but drug crime is increasing, more than any other type of crime, peaking in the years following the plan that was set into motion. (Figure 1). The amount of non violent drug offenders in prisons rose considerably, increasing to over 30 percent by 1990 from slightly over 10 percent in 1984, and fluctuating at about 33 percent of total admissions subsequently (Raphael, Stoll 2008).

        

In 1992, there were 1.3 million American being held in prison, within 20 years that number almost doubled (Waldman 2013). At the time of this article an astounding 52% of inmates in federal prisons had been admitted due to drug related offenses (Figure 2). In an article by Leon Neyfakh, the dramatic increase in both crime (violent and nonviolent) and mass incarceration of the people, directly to the war on drugs. In the article, according to professor John Pfaff of Fordham Law School, the war on drugs will never succeed due to it is based on a false understanding as to the uses, effects, and reasons as to why they are taken. (Neyfakh, 2009).
